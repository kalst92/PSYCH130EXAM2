Major Depressive Disorder
MDD · Chapter 4
▾
DSM-5-TR Criteria
  • ≥5 symptoms for ≥2 weeks
  • Must include: sad mood OR anhedonia
  • Sleep disturbance (insomnia or hypersomnia)
  • Psychomotor retardation or agitation
  • Weight/appetite change
  • Fatigue / loss of energy
  • Worthlessness or excessive guilt
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Recurrent thoughts of death or suicide
  • Symptoms cause significant distress/impairment
  • Not better explained by substance or medical condition
Epidemiology
  • Lifetime prevalence: ~16.2% in the U.S.
  • Twice as common in women as men
  • Gender gap emerges in early adolescence
  • Higher rates in poverty (3× more common)
  • ~15.8% of adolescents; 20% of college students affected
  • Age of onset has trended earlier over 60 years
  • 45% also meet criteria for anxiety disorder lifetime
  • Episodic — 40–50% recur within 10 years
Risk Factors — Biological
  • Heritability ~0.37 (moderate)
  • Elevated amygdala activity → threat hypersensitivity
  • Diminished striatal activity → blunted reward sensitivity
  • Diminished prefrontal cortex activity → poor emotion regulation
  • Pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-alpha) elevated
  • Circadian rhythm & sleep disruptions (insomnia)
  • Serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine dysregulation
Risk Factors — Psychological
  • Neuroticism: trait of frequent/intense negative affect
  • Beck's Cognitive Triad: negative schemas about self, world, future → info-processing biases
  • Hopelessness Theory: stable + global attributions for negative events → hopelessness → depression
  • Rumination: dwelling repetitively on sadness; women ruminate more → higher rates
  • Low reward sensitivity (behavioral & biological)
Risk Factors — Sociocultural
  • Childhood adversity (abuse, parental loss)
  • Recent negative life events (job loss, relationship loss)
  • Low social support (40% vs. 4% risk with/without confidant)
  • Income inequality, poverty
  • Cultural stigma, family cohesion
  • Gender minority status → elevated rates
Empirically Supported Treatments
  • Cognitive Therapy (CT) — challenge negative schemas; as effective as meds; protects against relapse
  • Interpersonal Psychotherapy (IPT) — targets interpersonal loss/conflict; as effective as CT
  • Behavioral Activation (BA) — increase rewarding activities; as effective as full CT
  • MBCT — mindfulness to prevent relapse
  • SSRIs / SNRIs — first-line meds; 60% show ≥50% symptom drop
  • ECT — most powerful biological treatment; used when meds fail
  • rTMS — option when antidepressants insufficient

Persistent Depressive Disorder
PDD (Dysthymia) · Chapter 4
▾
DSM-5-TR Criteria
  • Depressed mood most of the day, more days than not
  • Duration: ≥2 years (≥1 year in children/adolescents)
  • Symptoms never absent for >2 months at a time
  • ≥2 of: sleep disturbance, poor appetite/overeating, low energy, poor self-esteem, concentration difficulty, hopelessness
  • No manic or hypomanic episodes
Epidemiology
  • ~5% report depressive episodes >2 years
  • Also 2× more common in women
  • Average duration: ~10 years (highly chronic)
  • Outcomes often more severe than MDD due to chronicity
  • Highly comorbid with MDD ("double depression")
Key Distinction from MDD

MDD: ≥5 symptoms for ≥2 weeks (episodic). PDD: ≥2 symptoms for ≥2 years (chronic). PDD requires fewer symptoms but far longer duration. Chronicity is the primary predictor of poor outcome — PDD is not a "milder" disorder.

Treatments
  • Same as MDD: CT, IPT, BA, antidepressants
  • CBASP (Cognitive Behavioral Analysis System of Psychotherapy) specifically validated for chronic depression
  • Combination of therapy + meds often needed given chronicity

Bipolar I Disorder
BD-I · Chapter 4
▾
DSM-5-TR Criteria
  • ≥1 lifetime manic episode (required)
  • Manic episode: ≥1 week of distinctly elevated/irritable mood + increased energy/activity + ≥3 additional symptoms
  • Additional symptoms: inflated self-esteem, decreased need for sleep, pressured speech, flight of ideas, distractibility, increased goal-directed activity, risky behaviors
  • Significant impairment, hospitalization, or psychosis
  • Depression episode NOT required for diagnosis
Epidemiology
  • Prevalence ~1% in U.S. (rarer than MDD)
  • Equal rates in men and women
  • Women experience more depressive episodes within BD
  • Onset: >50% before age 25
  • Highly recurrent: >50% experience ≥4 episodes lifetime
  • 60% relapse in first 4 years after first episode
  • ~66% comorbid anxiety disorder; many comorbid SUD
  • Among most severe disorders; high suicidality
Risk Factors — Biological
  • Heritability ~0.93 (among most heritable disorders)
  • Genetic overlap with schizophrenia (shared SNPs)
  • Elevated amygdala & anterior cingulate activity
  • Elevated striatum & orbitofrontal cortex (reward) — contrast to MDD
  • Diminished prefrontal cortex activity
  • Pro-inflammatory cytokines elevated
  • Circadian rhythm & sleep disturbances (even during well periods)
  • High reward sensitivity / dopamine dysregulation
Risk Factors — Psychological & Social
  • High reward sensitivity → predicts mania onset
  • Goal-attainment life events trigger manic episodes
  • Sleep deprivation can trigger mania experimentally
  • Negative life events, neuroticism → depressive episodes
  • Low social support → depressive episodes
  • Diagnosis delay (~6 years after first episode)
  • Minorities disproportionately undertreated
Treatments — Biological
  • Lithium — best-researched mood stabilizer; 80% mild benefit; must monitor blood levels carefully
  • Anticonvulsants — e.g., Depakote; FDA-approved for acute mania
  • Antipsychotics — e.g., olanzapine; immediate calming; FDA-approved for bipolar depression
  • Combination usually needed; lifelong medication recommended
Treatments — Psychological
  • Psychoeducation — improves medication adherence, reduces relapse
  • CT — modified for BD, identifies early manic signs
  • Family-Focused Therapy — educates family, enhances communication
  • IPT adapted for BD — stabilizes social rhythms
  • Psychological tx better for depression > mania episodes
  • CBT for insomnia reduces both depression and mania

Bipolar II Disorder
BD-II · Chapter 4
▾
DSM-5-TR Criteria
  • ≥1 hypomanic episode (required)
  • ≥1 major depressive episode (required)
  • No lifetime manic episode (distinguishes from BD-I)
  • Hypomanic episode: ≥4 days, observable change, no marked impairment, no psychosis
Epidemiology & Key Facts
  • Prevalence: 0.4–2% (varied estimates)
  • Equal rates men/women
  • Often misdiagnosed as unipolar depression
  • Hypomania may feel pleasant — insight often impaired
Treatments
  • Lamotrigine — particularly effective for bipolar depression in BD-II; better tolerated than lithium for many
  • Lithium — also used; reduces depressive and hypomanic episodes
  • Atypical Antipsychotics — e.g., quetiapine; FDA-approved for bipolar depression
  • SSRIs / SNRIs — used cautiously; must be combined with a mood stabilizer to reduce risk of hypomanic switch
  • Psychoeducation, CT, IPT, Family-Focused Therapy — same adjunct psychological approaches as BD-I; especially helpful for depressive episodes
  • Lifelong medication generally recommended given high recurrence
BD-I vs. BD-II Key Difference

BD-I: Mania (severe, causes impairment, ≥1 week). Depression not required.
BD-II: Hypomania (milder, observable but not markedly impairing, ≥4 days). Major depression required. No manic episodes ever.

Cyclothymic Disorder
Cyclothymia · Chapter 4
▾
DSM-5-TR Criteria
  • ≥2 years (1 year in children) of chronic mood fluctuations
  • Numerous periods of hypomanic symptoms (not meeting full criteria)
  • Numerous periods of depressive symptoms (not meeting MDD criteria)
  • Symptoms present ≥50% of the time; never clear >2 months
  • No manic, hypomanic, or MDD episode ever met
Epidemiology
  • Estimated ~4% prevalence
  • Causes significant distress/functional impairment
  • Risk factor for developing full BD-I or BD-II
Treatments
  • Mood Stabilizers — lithium or valproate at lower doses; used to reduce cycling frequency and severity
  • Psychoeducation — mood monitoring, sleep/circadian hygiene, recognizing early warning signs of escalation
  • CT / Psychotherapy — adapted from BD treatment; helps manage mood fluctuations and prevent progression to full BD-I or BD-II
  • Avoid antidepressants alone — risk of triggering hypomanic/manic switch

Suicide & Suicidality
Chapter 4 — Critical Topic
▾
Key Terms
  • Suicidal ideation: thoughts of killing oneself
  • Suicide attempt: behavior intended to end life
  • Suicide: intentional act that ends one's life
  • NSSI: behaviors causing bodily harm without intent to die
Epidemiology
  • ~700,000 deaths/year worldwide
  • 9% lifetime suicidal ideation; 2.5% attempt
  • Men 4× more likely to die from suicide (U.S.)
  • Women more likely to attempt (non-lethal)
  • Guns: >50% of U.S. suicides
  • Elevated among ethnic/sexual minorities
  • Rates increasing in U.S./Americas; decreasing elsewhere
Risk Factors
  • Bio: Heritability ~50%; serotonin system disruption
  • Psych Disorder: MDD, BD, schizophrenia, SUD, PTSD, BPD all elevate risk (~6–8% of hospitalized die by suicide)
  • Social: divorce/widowhood (4× elevated), perceived burdensomeness, lack of belonging (Joiner's model)
  • Psych: hopelessness, poor problem-solving
  • Social contagion: celebrity suicides → 12% increase following month
  • Action vs. Ideation: blunted fear of bodily harm → more likely to act
Prevention & Treatment
  • CBT — safety planning; reduces hopelessness; halves re-attempt risk
  • DBT — specifically for recurrent self-harm/BPD; reduces suicidal behavior
  • Ketamine — same-day relief from suicidal ideation
  • Means restriction — coal gas example; bridge barriers; powerful public health tool
  • National hotline: 988 (2022)
  • Talk openly about suicide — does NOT increase risk

Specific Phobia
Chapter 5
▾
DSM-5-TR Criteria
  • Disproportionate, consistent fear of specific object/situation
  • Duration typically ≥6 months
  • Object/situation avoided or endured with intense anxiety
  • Types: Animal, Natural Environment, Blood/Injection/Injury, Situational, Other
  • Blood/injection/injury subtype: unique vasovagal fainting response
Epidemiology
  • 12-month prevalence: ~10.1%; Lifetime: ~13.8%
  • Most common specific anxiety disorder
  • Highly comorbid with other phobias
  • Onset typically childhood/adolescence
Risk Factors
  • Conditioning: Two-factor model — direct experience, modeling, verbal instruction
  • Bio: Prepared learning — evolutionary readiness to fear certain stimuli (snakes, heights, angry faces)
  • Psych: Neuroticism, behavioral inhibition
  • Bio: Heritability ~50–60%; amygdala hyperreactivity
Treatment
  • Exposure therapy (CBT) — most effective; graded hierarchy; can work in just hours
  • Virtual reality exposure effective as in-vivo
  • Minimal medication use; psychological treatment preferred

Social Anxiety Disorder
Social Phobia · Chapter 5
▾
DSM-5-TR Criteria
  • Marked, disproportionate fear of social scrutiny or exposure to unfamiliar people
  • Fear of acting in ways that will be embarrassing/humiliating
  • Social situations avoided or endured with intense anxiety
  • Typically ≥6 months
  • Most common fears: public speaking, meetings, meeting new people, authority figures
Epidemiology
  • 12-month: ~8%; Lifetime: ~13%
  • Onset typically in adolescence
  • More common in women
  • ≥1/3 also meet criteria for avoidant personality disorder
  • Comorbid with MDD (~60%), other anxiety disorders
Risk Factors
  • Conditioning: Two-factor model; safety behaviors perpetuate avoidance
  • Psych/Cognitive: Overly negative self-evaluation of social performance; excessive focus on internal cues vs. external social cues
  • Bio: Behavioral inhibition (strong predictor, 3.79× risk); amygdala hyperreactivity
  • Psych: Neuroticism; harsh self-evaluations predict increases over time
Treatment
  • CBT/Exposure — face feared social situations; reduce safety behaviors; social skill coaching
  • Group therapy — natural social exposure setting
  • SSRIs/SNRIs — evidence-based medications
  • Challenge overly negative social self-evaluations

Panic Disorder
Chapter 5
▾
DSM-5-TR Criteria
  • Recurrent unexpected panic attacks
  • ≥1 month of concern about attacks OR behavior change due to attacks
  • Panic attack: sudden intense fear with ≥4 symptoms (palpitations, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, chest pain, dizziness, derealization, depersonalization, fear of dying/going crazy)
  • Attacks peak within 10 minutes
  • NOT triggered by specific phobia situations
Epidemiology
  • 12-month: ~3.1%; Lifetime: ~5.2%
  • More common in women
  • Often leads to agoraphobia development
  • People often seek ER care (mistaking for cardiac event)
Risk Factors
  • Bio: Locus coeruleus → norepinephrine surges → sympathetic nervous system activation
  • Conditioning: Interoceptive conditioning — panic conditioned to internal bodily sensations
  • Psych/Cognitive: Catastrophic misinterpretation of somatic cues ("I'm having a heart attack")
  • Anxiety sensitivity: fear of one's own anxiety symptoms (Anxiety Sensitivity Index)
  • Bio: Slower extinction of conditioned fear; amygdala hyperactivity
Treatment
  • CBT with interoceptive exposure — deliberately induce bodily sensations in session (hyperventilation, spinning) to extinguish fear
  • Challenge catastrophic misinterpretations
  • SSRIs/SNRIs — first-line medications
  • Benzodiazepines — short-term only; risk of dependence
🏠
Agoraphobia
Chapter 5
▾
DSM-5-TR Criteria
  • Disproportionate fear/anxiety about ≥2 situations where escape would be difficult or help unavailable
  • Common situations: outside home alone, public transportation, open spaces, enclosed spaces, standing in line/crowd
  • Situations avoided, require companion, or endured with intense anxiety
  • Duration ≥6 months; causes significant distress/impairment
  • Recognized as distinct disorder only in DSM-5
Epidemiology & Risk
  • 12-month: ~1.7%; Lifetime: ~2.6%
  • Often comorbid with panic disorder
  • Risk: genetic vulnerability, life events
  • Severe cases: individuals may become housebound
Treatments
  • CBT with Exposure — primary treatment; graded in-vivo exposure to feared situations (crowds, public transport, open spaces); same core approach as panic disorder
  • Interoceptive Exposure — if comorbid panic disorder, address conditioned fear of bodily sensations alongside situational exposure
  • SSRIs / SNRIs — first-line medications; especially helpful when comorbid panic disorder is present
  • Benzodiazepines: short-term only due to dependence risk
  • Goal: eliminate avoidance and companion-dependence through repeated exposure without safety behaviors

Generalized Anxiety Disorder
GAD · Chapter 5
▾
DSM-5-TR Criteria
  • Excessive, uncontrollable worry about multiple topics
  • Present ≥50% of days for ≥6 months
  • ≥3 symptoms (1 in children): restlessness, fatigue, concentration difficulty, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance
  • Worry difficult to control
  • Causes significant distress/impairment
Epidemiology
  • 12-month: ~2.9%; Lifetime: ~6.2%
  • More common in women
  • Most likely anxiety disorder to cause marital distress
  • High comorbidity with MDD (shared genetic vulnerability)
  • Chronic, often poorly recognized
Risk Factors
  • Cognitive: Contrast avoidance model — worry reduces emotional volatility; people with GAD find sudden emotional shifts highly aversive
  • Cognitive: Intolerance of uncertainty
  • Bio: GABA, serotonin, norepinephrine dysfunction
  • Psych: Neuroticism, behavioral inhibition
  • Conditioning: Sustained negative beliefs about future; perceived lack of control
Treatment
  • CBT — exposure to feared outcomes; challenge negative beliefs; tolerate uncertainty
  • Relaxation training — progressive muscle relaxation, imagery
  • Mindfulness/Acceptance — manage distress without suppression
  • Unified Protocol — transdiagnostic approach for internalizing disorders
  • SSRIs/SNRIs — first-line medications
  • Benzodiazepines: no longer first-line (addictive, cognitive side effects)

📌 Cross-Cutting Risk Factors (Anxiety Disorders)

All anxiety disorders share: fear conditioning (Mowrer's two-factor model), slower extinction of conditioned fear, heightened amygdala reactivity, diminished medial prefrontal cortex activity, disruptions in GABA, serotonin, norepinephrine, neuroticism, behavioral inhibition, and cognitive styles including attention to threat, perceived lack of control, and intolerance of uncertainty. Women are ~2× more likely to experience anxiety disorders. Countries with war/persecution and high income inequality show higher prevalence.
